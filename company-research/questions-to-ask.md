# Questions to Ask

The goal is not to perform curiosity. The goal is to collect information that reveals fit, priorities, interview criteria, and hidden objections.

## Phone-screen priorities

Ask three, in this order when the conversation allows.

### 1. Discover the existing positive signal

> What aspects of my background stood out when the team reviewed my application?

Why it works: the answer reveals the argument already winning internally. Reinforce it in later rounds.

### 2. Clarify the role's real center of gravity

> How is this position divided between building data infrastructure, investigating system performance, and supporting field operations?

Why it works: it distinguishes the role from a generic analytics job and gives Josh language for the next round.

### 3. Reveal the next evaluation gate

> What would the next stage of the interview process evaluate?

Why it works: it converts the recruiter into a source of preparation intelligence.

## Strong recruiter alternatives

- What are the most important problems the person hired into this role would tackle first?
- How does the team define success during the first six months?
- How closely does this role work with hardware, autonomy, and field-test teams?
- How often does the role work in classified or network-isolated environments?
- Is the role being hired at one fixed level, or is leveling determined during the process?
- What does the team consider the hardest part of finding the right person for this position?

## Hiring-manager questions

- What decisions does the team currently struggle to make confidently because of data limitations?
- What is an example of a recent system-performance investigation this role supported?
- Where are the largest data-quality risks today: source systems, ingestion, modeling, ground truth, or user interpretation?
- How do architecture and developer workflow change between connected and network-isolated environments?
- How does the team balance low latency against completeness and correction of late-arriving data?
- Which users are expected to self-serve, and what prevents that today?
- What would you want the new hire to improve substantially within six months?
- Which part of the stack has the steepest learning curve for someone joining the team?

## Technical-peer questions

- How are software version, configuration, calibration, and mission context represented in the data model?
- How does the team handle replay, late events, and corrections to historical metrics?
- What does local development look like for classified or air-gapped deployments?
- How are data contracts and schema evolution managed across hardware and software teams?
- What testing strategy gives the team confidence in mission-critical transformations?
- Where do tools such as Foundry, Kafka, Flink, DuckDB, or Polars fit in the architecture?

## Strategic closing question

> Based on our conversation, is there any part of my background that would be helpful for me to clarify before we wrap up?

Use this only if delivered calmly. It is an opportunity to resolve uncertainty, not ask for reassurance.

## Questions to avoid during the first screen

- Anything answered clearly on the job posting
- Questions focused primarily on perks or time off
- "What does Anduril do?"
- Highly technical architecture questions the recruiter cannot answer
- Questions whose only purpose is to sound intelligent
- Asking five questions when the recruiter has time for two

## Capture after the call

Immediately write down:

- What they said stood out
- Exact next-round format
- Any concern or hesitation
- Team structure
- First problems and success criteria
- Tools named spontaneously
- Timeline and decision process
- Names of future interviewers
