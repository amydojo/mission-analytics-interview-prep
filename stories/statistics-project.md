# Story 5: Statistical Judgment

## Primary option: Mobile Health Study

### Known evidence

- Approximately 300 participants observed over 14 days
- Longitudinal physical-activity data
- Motivational messaging intervention
- Linear mixed-effects models
- Random intercepts and slopes
- Heterogeneous treatment effects

### Recruiter-level answer

> In my master's program, I analyzed repeated mobile-health observations from about 300 participants. Because each participant contributed multiple days of data, ordinary regression would have treated dependent observations as independent. I used mixed-effects models to separate differences in individual baselines from differences in treatment response, then translated the results into where tailored messaging appeared more useful than generic messaging.

## Secondary option: NBA Causal Analysis

Use this for a failure, judgment, or intellectual-honesty question.

### Core lesson

The project used a causal graph and decision tree to investigate factors associated with winning, but the final report recognized that the original causal framing did not fully answer the decision question. A policy-learning formulation would have aligned more directly with the desired action.

### Interview answer

> In a graduate project, my team used causal-graph methods and a classifier to study factors associated with NBA wins. The models produced interpretable patterns, but during evaluation I recognized that our causal formulation did not fully answer the decision question we had posed. We documented that limitation rather than overstating the results and proposed a policy-learning approach that would better match the objective. It reinforced that a sophisticated model is not automatically the right model; the method has to fit the actual decision.
